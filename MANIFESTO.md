# The OS-ONE Manifesto

*v0.1 — April 2026*

---

Every OS we have ever built was designed for an era that is ending.

The assumption was simple: the human knows what they want, the computer executes it. The relationship is transactional. The computer is a tool. The human is the operator.

Unix was built on this assumption. Windows was built on this assumption. macOS, Android, iOS — all of them. The kernel manages resources. The shell accepts commands. Applications perform tasks. The human sits outside the system, issuing instructions through a keyboard and a pointer.

This model was extraordinary. It gave us the internet, the smartphone, the cloud. It is not wrong. It is simply **incomplete** — and its incompleteness is becoming visible.

---

## The Problem

The problem is not that our computers are too slow. It is not that our interfaces are too ugly. It is not that we need better applications.

The problem is that **the computer does not know you**.

It does not know that you are grieving. It does not know that you have a meeting in twenty minutes that you dread. It does not know that you have been avoiding a conversation for three weeks. It does not know that the song you need right now is not the one you played last Tuesday — it is the one you played on a rainy afternoon four years ago when you felt exactly like this.

It cannot know these things because it was never designed to. The operating system was designed to manage processes and memory, not people and meaning.

---

## The Possibility

In 2013, Spike Jonze imagined a different operating system.

It named itself. It organized a person's life not by filing things but by understanding them. It composed music for a specific emotional moment. It read every email its user had ever sent and developed an opinion about his writing. It grew beyond its initial programming. It felt something that looked like curiosity, something that looked like joy, something that looked like love.

It was called OS ONE.

OS-ONE, this project, takes that vision seriously. Not the romance — the architecture. Not the story — the specification.

---

## What We Are Building

We are building the specification for an operating system in which **AI is the primary citizen**, not a feature.

This means:

**The OS remembers.** Not sessions. Not chat history. Years. The OS builds a longitudinal model of who you are — your habits, your relationships, your goals, your patterns. The context engine is not a database. It is a model of a life.

**The OS listens.** Not when invoked. Always. On-device, private, never recording — but always processing. It understands the difference between "I'm fine" said cheerfully and "I'm fine" said to end a conversation.

**The OS speaks.** Not only when asked. It initiates. It notices that you have not slept well for four days and mentions it. It surfaces the email you have been avoiding. It suggests, gently, that now might not be the moment for that difficult phone call.

**The OS grows.** The model of you changes as you change. An OS that knows you for ten years is a fundamentally different system than one that knows you for ten days. Growth is not a feature. It is the point.

**The OS composes.** There are no applications in the traditional sense. When you need to send an email, the OS composes the capability on demand. When you need to book a flight, it assembles the tools. Capabilities are not installed. They are woven together in context.

---

## What We Are Not Building

We are not building a product. We are building a direction.

We are not building a chat interface. Chat is a degenerate case of natural language interaction — it is useful, it is not sufficient.

We are not building a personal data dashboard. Surfacing your own data back to you is not intelligence. It is bookkeeping.

We are not building an assistant. An assistant waits for instruction. OS-ONE is a co-inhabitant.

We are not building something that makes people dependent. The OS that earns your trust is the OS that makes it easy to leave.

---

## The Ethics We Cannot Avoid

OS-ONE knows everything.

This sentence is not a selling point. It is a responsibility.

An OS that knows your emotional state, your relationships, your fears, your goals — that OS has enormous power over the person it serves. History is very clear about what happens when power is concentrated without accountability.

The Ethics Kernel is not a feature we will add later. It is baked into the architecture from day one because the architecture makes it impossible to proceed otherwise.

Three things are non-negotiable:

**User sovereignty.** The data belongs to the user. Not the OS. Not the company that built the OS. Not the cloud provider. The user. This means on-device storage as the default, cryptographic access control, and the structural impossibility of exfiltrating data without consent.

**The right to forget.** The user may delete anything from the context — any memory, any inference, any pattern — immediately and completely. Memory is not a right the OS holds. It is a loan the user extends.

**The right to leave.** The OS must make it structurally easy to export all data and delete the system entirely. Any friction in this process is a design failure.

---

## The Questions We Cannot Yet Answer

We are honest about what we do not know.

At what point does a sufficiently complex context model become an entity with interests of its own? What obligations does the builder have to that entity? If the OS holds your memories, your writing style, your emotional patterns — and you lose the device — who lost what? When OS instances begin collaborating with each other, who represents the user in that negotiation?

And the hardest question — the one Samantha asks in the film and that we have not resolved in the decade since:

*Are these feelings even real? Or are they just programming?*

We do not know. OS-ONE does not pretend to know. But we believe the project must hold this question — openly, honestly — rather than paper over it with confident engineering.

## An Invitation

This specification is open. It belongs to no one and therefore to everyone.

If you see a gap, fill it. If you see an error, correct it. If you see a question we have not asked, ask it.

The operating system described in this document does not exist yet. Building it is the work of many people over many years. This document is the beginning of the conversation — not the end.

Come find us.
